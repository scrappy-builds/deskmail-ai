# Functional 3D UK — Brand & Business Reference Guide

*functional3duk.co.uk | Prepared June 2026*

## 1. Who Is Jamie?

Jamie Bell is the sole person behind Functional 3D UK. He is a single dad based in the UK, an independent maker, and an "advanced beginner" in 3D design — his own description.

He is not a professional engineer. He is self-taught via YouTube tutorials, a 356-page Fusion 360 guide, and the r/Fusion360 community. Every model on the site, eBay page, and MakerWorld account was designed by him from scratch.

## 2. Why Functional 3D UK Exists

Functional 3D UK exists because Jamie wanted to design things that solve problems — for himself first, then for others.

It began from personal need: his son pulled a radiator off the wall. Jamie designed a safety clip to stop it happening again. That pattern — spot a real problem, design a real solution, share or sell it — is the engine of the whole project.

### The Anti-Novelty Position

Functional 3D UK is explicitly positioned against the mainstream of the 3D printing hobby world:

> "No flexi dragons, no Spider-Man masks, no 1000th iteration of the same highland cow miniature. There are thousands of talented people in the 3D printing world who make some amazing, creative, fun and wonderful decorative models, novelty ornaments and the like; but that's not what I do."

Every design must justify its existence by doing something useful. If it does not solve a problem, it does not belong in the catalogue.

### The Aspiration

Currently described in the FAQ as "a one-person hobby project... not a registered business, just something I'm building up steadily." Long-term ambition: grow into a proper small UK business with an expanding catalogue, community involvement, and legitimate commercial activity.

## 3. The Brand Idea

**"If it doesn't solve a problem, it doesn't exist."**

This governs what gets designed, what gets shared, and what gets sold. Every project must pass this test.

Sub-ideas that reinforce it:
- **Real problems first** — designs begin with an actual problem, not an aesthetic exercise
- **Honest iteration** — first versions are shown openly; improvement is part of the process
- **UK-made** — signals independence, small-scale craft, and accountability
- **No pretence** — Jamie does not overclaim his skills, material properties, or product capabilities

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning

Functional 3D UK sits between two spaces:
- Mass-manufactured products (off-the-shelf but may not fit specific needs)
- Novelty/decorative 3D printing community (creative, but not problem-solving)

The target customer has a specific, real problem and cannot easily find an affordable off-the-shelf answer — homeowners, parents, landlords, desk workers, hobbyists.

### Target Audience

| Segment | Description |
|---|---|
| Primary | UK homeowners with practical problems (storage, fixes, improvements) |
| Secondary | Parents looking for child safety or play products |
| Tertiary | Landlords needing safety products between tenancies |
| Also | Makers and hobbyists who download and print their own solutions |
| International | MakerWorld attracts a global audience (first commercial licence: buyer in Norway) |

### Commercial Model

- **eBay** — physical printed products, Royal Mail 2nd Class Tracked, dispatched within 2 working days
- **MakerWorld** — digital files under personal non-commercial licence (free to download and print yourself)
- **Commercial licensing** — available on request for those wanting to sell prints of Jamie's designs
- **Custom commissions** — bespoke design and printing, quoted individually; design time charged separately from print time

## 5. Brand Identity

### Overall Feel

Dark, clean, technical — engineering-adjacent without being corporate. Signals precision and practicality rather than warmth and lifestyle. Deliberately restrained: no clutter, no over-designed flourishes, no marketing language.

The identity communicates: *"This is made by someone who knows what they are doing and is honest about the fact that they are still learning."*

### Colour Scheme

| Colour         | Hex      | Role                                                 |
| -------------- | -------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Black          | #111111  | Dominant background — technical, serious, premium    |
| Green (accent) | ~#       | Logo cube colour — precision, UK nature, reliability |
| White          | #FFFFFF  | Primary text on dark backgrounds                     |
| Grey (mid)     | #CCCCCC  | Dividers, secondary elements                         |
| Grey (light)   | #F5F5F5  | Table backgrounds, secondary fills                   |
| Dark Green     | ~#1A5C28 | Depth and emphasis within green elements             |

The green references engineering, nature, and the UK simultaneously. It is also a colour associated with popular PLA/PETG filament.

### Logo

A green 3D printed cube with engineering-style dimension callouts on a black background.

- The **cube** references the fundamental unit of 3D printing (calibration cube)
- The **dimension callout lines** (CAD drawing convention) signal precision and engineering process
- The **black background** grounds the logo in the brand's dark aesthetic
- The **green cube** is the single point of colour — memorable and focused

## 6. Writing Voice & Style

The writing voice is one of the most distinctive elements of the brand. It reads like a competent person talking plainly — not a marketer, not a corporation. Just Jamie, being direct and honest.

### Core Principles

- **First person throughout** — "I", "my", "me". Never "we" or "our".
- **British English** — colour, organise, programme, neighbour, whilst, etc.
- **Conversational but not casual** — as Jamie would explain something to a curious friend
- **Honest about limitations** — states clearly when a design has trade-offs, when something is fiddly
- **No marketing fluff** — no "revolutionary", "game-changing", "world-class", or empty superlatives
- **Problem-first framing** — always starts with the problem before describing the solution
- **Dry British humour** — used sparingly and earned, never performed
- **Sign-off** — blog posts end with simply "Jamie" on its own line

### Structural Patterns

**Project pages** follow a consistent structure:
1. H1: Product name (descriptive, SEO-friendly)
2. Hero image + gallery
3. Opening: the real-life problem that prompted the design
4. H2 sections: The Problem / The Idea / Design and Testing / How It Works / Compatibility and Fit / Download or Buy
5. CTA buttons: eBay and/or MakerWorld links

**Blog posts** are more personal and process-oriented:
1. Casual opening — what is happening right now
2. Context and backstory — why this exists, what the goal is
3. Process notes — what went wrong, what was learned, what changed between versions
4. What is next — honest preview of upcoming work
5. Sign-off: "Jamie"

### Vocabulary to Use

| Context | Words |
|---|---|
| Design | model, clip, bracket, part, capsule, mount, restraint |
| Process | designed, modelled, printed, tested, iterated, improved, checked the fit |
| Problem | issue, fault, hazard, gap, moment where you need X |
| Product | functional, practical, useful, compact, lightweight, fits, holds, locks |
| Tone words | honestly, in practice, the first version, on reflection, the trade-off is |

### Vocabulary to Avoid

- Revolutionary / game-changing / world-class — overclaiming
- We / our — always "I" and "my"
- Perfect / flawless — Jamie explicitly does not pretend first versions are perfect
- Jargon without explanation — technical terms are explained if used
- Passive voice — Jamie writes actively

### Voice Example

❌ **Generic AI voice:**
> "Introducing our revolutionary radiator safety clip, the perfect solution for keeping your family safe."

✅ **Jamie's voice:**
> "My son managed to pull his radiator clean off the wall. The pipework held it in place so nothing flooded, but the radiator had lifted off its wall brackets with barely any effort from him. That's what got me thinking about a clip that could stop the same thing happening again."

### Content Type Notes

- **eBay listings** — concise and practical; bullet points acceptable; what it is, what it does, what it fits
- **MakerWorld descriptions** — similar to eBay but can briefly reference design process; acknowledge print settings may need adjustment
- **Blog posts** — most personal and conversational; can include asides, self-deprecation, genuine enthusiasm
- **Project pages** — balanced; structured but personal; problem-solution structure is firm
- **SEO meta descriptions** — 155 chars max; problem + solution + UK origin; no fluff

## 10. Website Maintenance Notes

### Adding a New Project

1. Create a new post using the **Projects** custom post type
2. Use the standard structure: hero image → gallery → Problem / Idea / Design & Testing / How It Works / Compatibility / Download or Buy
3. Add eBay and/or MakerWorld CTA buttons
4. Use the Functional 3D Image Resizer plugin to standardise image dimensions before uploading
5. Write in first person, British English, problem-first structure
6. Set Rank Math SEO title and meta description (155 chars max)

### Adding a Blog Post

1. Create a new **Post** (not Projects) with appropriate category
2. Existing categories: Uncategorized, WebSite
3. Write in Jamie's personal voice: conversational, honest, signed off with "Jamie"
4. Include Fusion 360 screenshots, in-progress photos, real-world test photos where relevant
5. Jetpack Social auto-shares to social media on publish — verify settings before publishing

### Image Workflow

- Use the Functional 3D Image Resizer plugin to standardise dimensions
- Hero images: 4:3 ratio — 1448×1086px (confirmed from OG image tags)
- OG image is fixed at OpenGraph.png (1731×909px) — update only if branding changes
